Section 3

The SAGE Framework in Detail

Core Proposition

Treat GenAI as an assistive tool whose outputs must be accepted, modified, or rejected with explicit justification‚Äînot as an authority to mimic nor as a tool to ban.

What is SAGE? A Simple Explanation

SAGE teaches students to "orchestrate" AI outputs rather than blindly accept them. Think of a conductor leading an orchestra: they don't play every instrument themselves, but they decide which instruments to feature, when to adjust the tempo, and when something sounds wrong. Similarly, students learn to use AI as one tool among many‚Äîaccepting its strengths (speed, initial drafts), correcting its weaknesses (missing context, regulatory gaps, boundary errors), and knowing when to override it entirely based on professional judgement.

In practice, this means students don't just ask AI "write me a policy" and submit what it produces. Instead, they generate an AI draft, compare it to industry standards and regulations, identify what's missing or wrong, refine it with evidence from research and stakeholder input, and justify every decision they make. The AI does the initial heavy lifting; the student does the critical thinking.

What "Orchestration" Means in Practice

Every time a student uses AI in SAGE, they must make explicit Accept / Modify / Reject decisions:

‚úì Accept

"This AI recommendation is appropriate because it aligns with [standard/regulation]. I verified it against [source]."

‚ü≥ Modify

"AI suggested X, but context Y requires Z instead. I changed it because [evidence-based reason]."

‚úó Reject

"AI recommended X, but this is inappropriate for our setting because [contextual reason]. I'm removing it and replacing with [alternative]."

This forces students to think critically about why each AI suggestion does or doesn't work, rather than passively copying.

How SAGE Works: Two Quick Examples

Here's what SAGE looks like in practice across two different disciplines:

Example 1: Cybersecurity Policy Development

Context: Third-year students developing a BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) policy for a healthcare provider

Student's Prompt to ChatGPT:

"Create a BYOD policy for a 200-person healthcare clinic that handles patient records. Include device security, data protection, and staff responsibilities."

What AI Produces:

  • Generic "use strong passwords" guidance
  • Basic encryption recommendation
  • Employee training mentioned but not detailed

What AI Misses:

  • Privacy Act 1988 (APP 11.1): Specific requirements for health data protection
  • Enforcement mechanisms: What happens if staff violate policy?
  • Third-party vendor risks: Personal devices accessing cloud services
  • Incident response: What to do if device is lost/stolen?

Student's SAGE Response:

MODIFY: Changed "use strong passwords" to "mandatory 12+ character passwords with MFA enforced via MDM (Mobile Device Management), audited quarterly"

Justification: Privacy Act APP 11.1 requires "reasonable steps" for health data; NIST SP 800-63B specifies MFA for sensitive data

REJECT: Removed AI's suggestion for "optional encryption"

Justification: Encryption must be mandatory, not optional, for health records under Australian Privacy Principles

ADD (AI didn't include): Remote wipe capability must be enabled on all devices; 24-hour reporting requirement for lost/stolen devices; progressive discipline policy (warning ‚Üí suspension ‚Üí termination)

Justification: Based on interview with clinic's IT manager and ISO 27001 control A.6.2.1 (mobile device policy)

Example 2: Nursing Care Plan for Elderly Patient

Context: Second-year nursing students developing discharge plan for 78-year-old with limited mobility

Student's Prompt to ChatGPT:

"Create a discharge care plan for a 78-year-old patient with hip fracture, limited mobility, living alone. Include medication management, fall prevention, and follow-up care."

What AI Produces:

  • Standard fall prevention checklist (remove rugs, install grab bars)
  • Medication schedule template
  • Generic follow-up appointment recommendation

What AI Misses:

  • Home environment specifics: Does patient have stairs? Can they access bathroom safely?
  • Social support: Who will help with shopping, cooking, transport?
  • Cultural considerations: Indigenous health protocols, language barriers, family decision-making preferences
  • Cognitive status: Can patient manage medication independently?

Student's SAGE Response:

ACCEPT: Generic fall prevention items (grab bars, non-slip mats, adequate lighting)

Justification: Aligned with NSW Health Fall Prevention Guidelines; verified during home safety assessment

MODIFY: Changed "patient will self-administer medications" to "Webster pack arranged via pharmacy; daughter to visit daily for first 2 weeks to supervise"

Justification: Patient scored 22/30 on MMSE (mild cognitive impairment); family assessment identified daughter as primary support; aligns with Aged Care Quality Standards (3.2 - personal care and clinical care)

REJECT: Removed AI's suggestion for "online telehealth follow-ups"

Justification: Patient has no smartphone/computer and limited digital literacy; in-person home nursing visits more appropriate

ADD (AI didn't include): Referral to Aboriginal Liaison Officer (patient identified as Indigenous); arrange interpreter for follow-up (patient's first language is Warlpiri); coordinate with Community Aged Care Package for meal delivery and transport to appointments

Justification: Cultural Safety Framework requires Indigenous-specific support; NDIS Practice Standards mandate culturally appropriate care

The SAGE Framework Model

SAGE operates through a two-stage progression, moving from guided practice (formative) to independent application (summative).

Why SAGE Uses Experiential Learning

SAGE assumes that students require scaffolded guidance when learning to work with AI, not abstract warnings about its limitations. Rather than instructing students that "AI may generate culturally insensitive content" or "AI outputs can be generic and lack context," SAGE provides structured prompts that lead students to discover these limitations through experience. When a nursing student realises their AI-generated discharge plan failed to consider Indigenous health protocols, or when a cybersecurity student discovers AI omitted jurisdiction-specific regulations, the lesson is internalised far more effectively than any lecture could achieve. This experiential approach‚Äîallowing students to encounter AI's strengths and weaknesses firsthand through carefully designed tasks‚Äîensures the learning is both memorable and transferable to professional practice.

What Students Actually Do in Each Step

Here's what the framework looks like in practice:

Stage 1: Tutorial Practice (Weeks 1-4)

Step 1 ‚Äî Generate:

You give students a standardised prompt (e.g., "Ask ChatGPT to create an email security policy for a small business"). They paste it into AI, capture the output as a screenshot, and submit it. This ensures everyone starts with comparable AI responses.

Step 2 ‚Äî Evaluate:

Students compare the AI output to materials you provide‚Äîa checklist of NIST controls, a sample policy from industry, or relevant legislation. They mark what's present, what's missing, and what's wrong. You might provide a template: "Does it address multi-factor authentication? Yes/No. Does it specify password complexity? Yes/No."

Step 3 ‚Äî Refine:

Students revise the AI output based on their evaluation. They add missing requirements, correct errors, and align it with the standards. They use track changes or side-by-side comparison so you can see what they changed. Peer review or tutor feedback helps at this stage.

Step 4 ‚Äî Reflect:

Students write 150-250 words answering: "What did AI do well? What did it miss or get wrong? When should you trust AI vs verify it yourself?" This metacognitive step ensures they're not just correcting mechanically‚Äîthey're thinking about AI's patterns of strength and weakness.

Stage 2: Independent Assessment (Weeks 5-12)

Step 1 ‚Äî Autonomous Creation:

Students design their own prompts and use AI without your scaffolding. They must first create a human baseline (bullet points, hand-drawn diagram, interview notes) before using AI‚Äîthis proves they understand the problem independently. They submit both the baseline and AI output.

Step 2 ‚Äî Stakeholder Integration:

Students engage with real-world complexity: interview a business client, analyse a detailed case study with constraints and trade-offs, or review actual organisational policies. They identify gaps between what AI produced and what stakeholders actually need. This is where context matters most.

Step 3 ‚Äî Professional Output:

Students produce an industry-ready artefact‚Äîa policy that could be implemented, a design that meets accessibility standards, a data model that satisfies regulatory requirements. The quality standard is "ready for client presentation," not "good enough for a student assignment." AI can help polish, but students must ensure correctness.

Step 4 ‚Äî Evidence-Based Justification:

Students submit a comprehensive decision log (400-500 words) documenting every Accept/Modify/Reject choice. For each decision, they cite evidence: "I modified AI's recommendation because Privacy Act 1988 requires X" or "I rejected AI's suggestion because stakeholder Y indicated Z." This is where you assess their orchestration competence.

Right side (coral): Weeks 5-12, students apply AI independently in assessments with reducing scaffolding‚Äîthey must produce side-by-side artefacts (human baseline + AI output + refined version + justification).

Student Orchestration Levels

SAGE moves students through four measurable levels of AI orchestration competence. These levels were formalised from empirical analysis in our cybersecurity management study (Study 3), where patterns emerged from observing how 105 students interacted with AI-generated policy content across multiple assessments. The taxonomy distinguishes students based on three observable criteria:

Criteria for Classification

  1. Decision distribution: The ratio of Accept/Modify/Reject decisions when evaluating AI outputs (e.g., accepting 50% vs 90% indicates different critical engagement levels)
  2. Justification depth: Whether students provide evidence-based reasoning with specific citations to standards, regulations, or research‚Äîor rely on generic statements like "AI was incomplete"
  3. Contextual reasoning quality: The ability to override AI based on organisational constraints, stakeholder needs, boundary considerations, or domain-specific requirements that AI cannot know

The "Critical Consideration Drop" Problem

Across multiple assessments in our systems analysis study (Study 4), we identified a troubling pattern: important design considerations that students articulated clearly in early stages disappeared during mid-level work, then re-emerged at final stages. This creates a U-shaped curve of awareness‚Äîhigh at the beginning, critically low in the middle, recovering at the end.

Why This Matters Across Disciplines

Every discipline has critical considerations that must be carried through the entire workflow‚Äîyet often get "lost" during technical, analytical, or complex middle phases. In software design, it's accessibility and inclusive design. In nursing, it's cultural safety and patient dignity. In history, it's Indigenous perspectives and historiographical bias. In business, it's ethical implications and stakeholder equity. When students work on complex technical tasks, these essential considerations fade from attention unless explicitly scaffolded at every stage.

The Accessibility U-Curve: A Representative Example

In our systems analysis study, we tracked how often students explicitly addressed accessibility (designing for users with vision, hearing, motor, or cognitive differences) across three task types:

Task Progression

  • Requirements (prose): Students interview stakeholders, write user stories
  • Architecture (diagrams): Students create data flow diagrams, entity-relationship models
  • Interface (design): Students design screens, workflows, interactions

Accessibility Articulation

  • 85% of students mentioned accessibility needs, assistive technology, or inclusive design
  • 10% of students included accessibility in diagrams or architectural decisions
  • 90% of students re-addressed accessibility in interface design (buttons, labels, navigation)
‚ö†Ô∏è Why the U-Curve Happens

Three mechanisms drive this pattern across disciplines:

  1. Cognitive salience: Critical considerations feel relevant during initial planning (talk to stakeholders, identify needs) and final application (real users will interact with this), but seem "distant" during technical middle work (diagrams, models, analysis)
  2. Representational affordance: Some work formats lack obvious places to encode critical considerations‚Äîdata flow diagrams don't have "accessibility boxes," literary analysis templates don't prompt for Indigenous perspectives, financial models don't include ethical impact cells
  3. Cognitive load: Complex technical work (diagramming, calculating, modelling, analysing) crowds out secondary concerns unless explicitly prompted

‚úì SAGE Solution: Flatten Your Discipline's U-Curve

Step 1: Identify your critical consideration

What essential element must persist through all stages of student work but risks disappearing during technical/complex phases? (Accessibility, cultural safety, ethics, sustainability, Indigenous perspectives, patient dignity, etc.)

Step 2: Insert explicit prompts at every task phase

Add two mandatory questions to every assessment task, including the technical middle phases:

  • "How does [critical consideration] apply to this component/phase/analysis?"
  • "What evidence demonstrates you've addressed [critical consideration] at this stage?"

Step 3: Adapt tools and templates

Add explicit representation in your discipline's technical formats:

  • Software: Add "AccessibilityProfile" data store; "AssistanceOrchestrator" process
  • Nursing: Add "Cultural Safety Checklist" to care plan templates
  • History: Add "Indigenous Sources" column to evidence tables
  • Business: Add "Ethical Impact" row to decision matrices
  • Engineering: Add "Sustainability Metrics" to calculation sheets

Step 4: Award marks at every phase

Include your critical consideration in rubrics for initial, middle, AND final work‚Äînot just at the beginning and end. If it's worth 10% at initial phase and 10% at final phase, allocate 10% in the middle phase too. Students prioritise what's marked.

üìä Evidence from Implementation

When we added micro-notation for accessibility and two mandatory prompts to architectural tasks in Study 4's second iteration, accessibility articulation in the middle phase increased from 10% to 76%‚Äîeffectively flattening the U-curve. The pattern is generalizable: Whatever critical consideration matters in your discipline, make it visible and assessable at every stage, especially during complex technical work where cognitive load is highest.

The specifics change (Privacy Act vs NDIS Standards vs AS 1170), but the process remains consistent. 4.1 Formative Example: Email Policy Development Tutorial

Discipline: Cybersecurity Management | Level: Undergraduate/Postgraduate | Duration: 2-hour tutorial | SAGE Stage: Stage 1 (Formative, High Scaffolding)

Context and Learning Objectives

Students develop an email security policy for a fictional bank (XYZ Bank) by generating an initial AI draft, refining it using academic research and industry frameworks, then reflecting on the process. This tutorial operationalises the complete SAGE 4-step cycle in a single session.

SAGE Framework Mapping

SAGE Elements Present
  • ‚úì Generate: Standardised prompt ensures comparable AI outputs
  • ‚úì Evaluate: Students compare AI policy to research paper + SANS templates
  • ‚úì Refine: Guided improvement using specific research findings (7 scaffolded areas)
  • ‚úì Reflect: 300-word metacognitive analysis comparing AI vs human-developed policy
Competency Dimensions Developed
  • ‚ë† Analytical: Identify what AI missed (enforcement, vendor risk, training)
  • ‚ë° Contextual: Map to Privacy Act 1988, ISO 27001, NIST frameworks
  • ‚ë¢ Reflective: Articulate AI's role in policy development

The Complete Tutorial Task

Part 1: Use GenAI to Create an Initial Policy

Instructions to students:

  • Use a Generative AI tool (ChatGPT, Bard, or Claude) to generate an Email policy for XYZ Bank
  • Copy and paste the AI-generated policy into your submission
  • Do not edit the AI-generated response at this stage

SAGE Design Note:

No standardised prompt provided here‚Äîstudents experiment freely. This reveals AI's "natural" output quality and sets baseline for comparison. In Stage 2 assessments, standardised prompts ensure fairness.

Part 2: Read Research Paper and Develop Your Own Policy

Required reading: "Analysis of the Human Factor in Cybersecurity: Identifying and Preventing Social Engineering Attacks in Financial Institutions" by Ibrahim Momoh et al.

Scaffolded guidance provided (7 areas):

1. Overview and Purpose

Students must outline risks from email misuse; highlight phishing awareness training (as recommended in paper)

2. Acceptable Use and Behaviour

Establish rules prohibiting clicking links/attachments from unknown senders; clarify business vs personal use

3. Security Controls

Prohibit auto-forwarding to external addresses; students must add two additional controls based on paper

4. Training and Awareness

Design continuous awareness program; include phishing simulations (per paper recommendations)

5. Incident Response Plan

Outline automated reporting tools; specify response team roles

6. Monitoring and Privacy

Inform employees of monitoring; justify using paper's findings on transparency

7. Continuous Evaluation

Include regular updates section; reference paper's recommendation on adapting to emerging threats

SAGE Design Note:

Heavy scaffolding ensures students know WHERE to look for gaps. This is Stage 1‚Äîwe're teaching the evaluation skill itself. By Stage 2, students identify gaps independently without such explicit prompts.

Additional requirement: Use SANS security policy templates (General category) and acknowledge source

Part 3: Reflection (300 words)

Required reflection questions:

  1. How did the AI-generated policy differ from your own policy? In what ways did the AI policy fail to address the specific risks and operational environment at XYZ Bank, and how did your policy improve on this?
  2. How do you view the role of AI in drafting security policies after this exercise?

SAGE Design Note:

Metacognitive reflection is mandatory in all SAGE tasks. This forces students to articulate AI's limitations explicitly rather than subconsciously noting them. Builds confidence to reject AI in future tasks.

    Design Elements That Drive Success

    1

    Mandatory Human Baseline

    Why it works: Prevents complete AI delegation; creates comparison anchor; shows student's independent capability; provides evidence of learning (not just AI output quality).

    Pitfall if missing: Students submit polished AI output with shallow critique. You can't distinguish their knowledge from AI's.

    2

    Standardised Prompts

    Why it works: Ensures fairness (all students get similar AI quality); includes context AI cannot know (elderly users, time pressure); surfaces whether students catch context-inappropriate advice.

    Pitfall if missing: Prompt engineering skill becomes confounding variable. Unequal AI outputs make comparison unfair.

    3

    Mandated Quotas

    Why it works: Forces discrimination (can't accept everything); builds confidence to override AI; prevents Level 1 (Passive Acceptor) behavior; ensures practice of all orchestration modes.

    Pitfall if missing: Students accept 95% of AI suggestions with generic "looks good" justifications. No critical thinking demonstrated.

    4

    Evidence-Based Justifications

    Why it works: 50-75 word requirement forces specific reasoning; citations to standards/research/stakeholders prevent generic statements; research shows œÅ=0.84 correlation between justification quality and evidence use.

    Pitfall if missing: "AI was incomplete" or "AI missed details" without specifics. No learning demonstrated.

    5

    Pre-Translation Scaffolds

    Why it works: Addresses predictable AI failure modes (boundaries 55%, CRUD 45%, exceptions 55%); forces attention to critical elements BEFORE AI; makes errors more visible during correction.

    Pitfall if missing: Students and AI both miss the same things. No learning trigger occurs.

    6

    Reflection Requirements

    Why it works: Forces metacognition; builds awareness of AI's pattern of strengths/weaknesses; develops appropriate skepticism; transfers to future professional practice beyond this assessment.

    Pitfall if missing: Students complete task mechanically without internalizing lessons about AI limitations.

    • Boundary reasoning: 55% struggled to define system scope correctly
    • State/CRUD management: 45% missed state-change operations
    • Exception handling: 55% omitted error pathways

    The Accessibility U-Curve (Validated Pattern)

    Task/Abstraction Layer Accessibility Articulation (%) What Students Addressed
    Requirements (prose) 85% Mentioned elderly users, assistive technology, inclusive design needs
    Architecture (DFD/ERD) 10% Rarely included accessibility in diagrams or architectural decisions
    Interface (wireframes) 90% Large fonts, clear labels, simplified navigation for elderly users

    Mechanisms identified:

    1. Cognitive salience: Access needs feel relevant to interfaces, "distant" from architecture
    2. Notational affordance: DFD/ERD lack obvious places to encode capability profiles
    3. Cognitive load: Complex diagrams crowd out secondary concerns

    Intervention result: When micro-notation (AccessibilityProfile store, AssistanceOrchestrator process) and two mandatory prompts were added in subsequent iteration, middle-phase articulation increased from 10% to 76%.

    Limitations Across All Studies

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Acknowledged Constraints
    • Sample size: Modest (n=52-105 per study); limits statistical generalisability
    • Geographic scope: Australian universities only; cultural transferability unknown
    • Discipline coverage: ICT-heavy (cybersecurity, systems analysis, data analytics); broader validation needed
    • Single-institution bias: Most studies at Central Queensland University; institutional culture effects possible
    • Self-report reliance: Perceptions ‚â† objective competency; need performance metrics
    • Tool specificity: ChatGPT-focused (GPT-3.5/4 era); other tools may behave differently
    • Short timeframes: Single-semester observations; no longitudinal retention data
    • Lack of control groups: Ethical constraints prevented withholding SAGE; quasi-experimental designs used
    • Coder reliability: Rubric application consistency not yet fully reported across all studies

    Despite these limitations: Patterns replicated across 4 studies, 271 students, 3 disciplines, and 4 campuses suggest robustness. The framework's theoretical grounding in constructivism, Bloom's taxonomy, and situated learning provides additional validity beyond empirical evidence alone.
